About Me

In June 2019 I graduated from MIT with a bachelor's degree from the Department of Mechanical Engineering. My flexible degree program allowed me to concentrate in something I'm passionate about— robotics— as well as the intersection of humans and technology. My fields of interest include hardware engineering, product design/management, and manufacturing, as well as a multitude of other areas. I've been in and out of various fabrication labs since grade school, and I love making things!

On campus, I was involved in TechX (where I was the director of ProjX, a group that provides funding for students' side projects); the Society of Women Engineers; my dorm's house government; student theater; and associate advising.

I was born and raised in Milwaukee, Wisconsin, which has given me an (un)healthy obsession with cheese. In my free time I enjoy reading, going on Wikipedia binges, and tinkering. I am a strong proponent of the Oxford comma.

Talon

In the class 2.009 - Product Engineering Processes, I helped develop Talon, a utility knife that automatically retracts when it contacts skin, as part of a team of 24 students. Talon uses capacitive sensing to detect when skin contacts the blade, and fires a solenoid which allows the blade to retract into the housing, preventing serious injury. We iterated through all phases of the design process-- from brainstorming ideas to sketch modeling to prototyping, finally choosing an idea, and designing and fabricating the final alpha prototype. Over the course of the semester, I was responsible for many aspects of the product, ranging from mechanism design to user testing to creating CAD renderings and animations. Talon is patent-pending and was successfully presented to over 40,000 viewers at the 2.009 product launch. See more details at the links below!

"Yonut" - Injection Molded Donut Yo-Yo

In the class 2.008 - Design & Manufacturing II, I helped design and manufacture 50 injection molded and thermoformed yo-yos in a team of 6. We chose to make a donut-themed yo-yo, which consisted of a donut top half, a donut bottom half, and frosting, all sitting on a plate. Over the course of the semester we designed the parts and their molds (or die, in the case of the thermoformed frosting part), injection molded (or thermoformed) the parts, and assembled 50 yo-yos. We learned about CAM, the mechanics of machining, process physics of injection molding, as well as in-depth analysis of additive manufacturing (FDM, SLA, and SLS methods), casting (sand, die, and lost-wax methods), robotics, cost, manufacturing systems, electronics, and more. Check out our blog at the link below to follow our yo-yo progress!

Loft for Dorm Room

In Fall 2018, I moved to a new dorm, and decided I wanted more space and a bigger bed. I created the room and all the stock furniture in CAD (SolidWorks) and designed a custom loft to fit within the space. The loft is big enough for a full or queen sized mattress, and fits my futon underneath. Because of the limited space available between the radiator and the wall, the bottom footprint had to be smaller than the top. I did FEA simulations within SolidWorks to make sure the loft would not tip over if someone hung from the edge.

I obtained leftover wood from my dorm's rush projects, and cut, sanded, and assembled the loft (with a little help lifting and moving). Then, I stained the wood gray and polyurethaned the heavy-use areas. The endeavour took a day to design and a few days to build, spread over a period of a few weeks due to other time commitments. I ended up moving to a different (bigger) room in Spring 2019, so I disassembled and reassembled the loft in my new room (with help from some friends). Photos are of the loft in my new room.

SolidWorks, CAD, loft, dorm room, wood, fun

Assistive Robot

In the class 2.12, Introduction to Robotics, my team of 12 was tasked with building a robot for a design challenge-- to assist a hemiplegic stroke patient with specific household tasks. As part of the human-robot communication subteam, I worked with 3 other students to determine how the patient would interact with the robot, and create the modules necessary to facilitate those interactions. We designed a system such that, using a wearable wrist device, the patient could send commands to the robot's onboard computer using voice and gestures. The wearable used an Arduino microcontroller, a voice recognition shield, and an inertial measurement unit (IMU) to receive and process commands, and sent those commands to the robot via a wireless RF serial connection that I set up. I also designed and 3D printed a case for the wearable. In addition to the work I did for the class competition, through class labs I gained experience with ROS, vehicle navigation, collision avoidance, computer vision, and simulation.

Robotics, Arduino, ROS, Computer Vision, Kinect
